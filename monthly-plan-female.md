### Why the programme is structured this way

Fat loss requires a sustained caloric deficit. However, without adequate protein and progressive resistance training, the body loses muscle alongside fat — a result that slows metabolism, reduces strength, and makes regaining fat more likely. This plan targets a modest deficit of 300–350 kcal/day, which supports roughly 0.5 kg of fat loss per week while preserving — and building — lean tissue.

The programming choices are deliberate:

- **McGill-safe core work** (bird dog, McGill curl-up, side plank, dead bug) replaces traditional sit-ups and crunches, which load the lumbar spine in flexion. These alternatives are backed by Dr Stuart McGill's research and are far more effective at building spinal endurance without aggravating the lower back.
- **Knee loading is graduated**: goblet squat is introduced before split squat; lateral band walks and clamshells are non-negotiable warm-up staples every lower body session to activate hip abductors and reduce knee valgus under load.
- **Hip flexor and glute work** are treated as injury prevention, not optional extras. Tight hip flexors (especially psoas) contribute to anterior pelvic tilt and lower back pain. Strong glutes offload both the lumbar spine and the knee.
- **Turkish Get-Up (TGU)** is included from Week 1. Your three years of yoga and Pilates are an asset here — TGU requires the exact kind of body awareness, breath control, and segmental stability that Pilates builds. You will pick this up faster than most beginners. Start light (4–6 kg) and treat it as a moving meditation rather than a strength exercise until the sequence is seamless.
- **Rucking on Thursday** delivers Zone 2 cardiovascular training and bone density stimulus without the knee impact of running. Both benefits are particularly relevant for women approaching perimenopause, where oestrogen decline accelerates bone loss.
- **Sunday family swimming** is counted as Zone 1–2 active recovery — no structure needed, just enjoy it.

## 5. The 4 Training Days (Detailed)

### Day A — Lower Body + KB Swing
**Monday, 7:30–8:45 am**
**Purpose:** Posterior chain development (swings), knee-safe squat pattern, hip hinge, knee and hip prehabilitation, McGill-safe core training.
**Movement patterns:** Hinge, Squat, Core.

#### Warm-Up (10 min)
A longer warm-up is warranted given the knee and lower back history. Do not skip this.

- **Glute bridge** — 2×15: Activates the glutes before loading. Lay on your back, feet flat, drive hips to ceiling, squeeze at the top for 1 sec.
- **Clamshell** — 2×12/side: Hip abductor activation. Reduces knee valgus (inward collapse) under load. Use a light band around the knees if available.
- **Bodyweight goblet squat** — 2×10: Practice the pattern before loading. Hold hands at chest. Push knees out, sit to comfortable depth.
- **Ankle dorsiflexion wall stretch** — 30 sec/side: Limited ankle mobility causes knee forward travel to compensate — address this.
- **Cat-cow** — 10 reps: Warm up lumbar and thoracic spine gently.

#### Main Work

1. **KB Swing**
   - Key cues: "Hike" the KB back between your legs (like hiking a football), then drive through the heels and snap the hips forward explosively. The arms are just a pendulum — the power comes from the hips. Think "crush the ground" at the bottom. This is NOT a squat — the hips hinge back, not down.
   - The swing is the single best posterior chain conditioning exercise in this programme. It simultaneously trains hip hinge power, glute strength, and cardiovascular conditioning.

2. **KB Goblet Squat**
   - Hold the KB at chest height with both hands cupping the bell. This front load forces an upright torso — far kinder to the lumbar spine than a back squat. Push the knees out over the toes. Depth to comfortable range; do not force range past the point where your lower back rounds.

3. **DB Romanian Deadlift (RDL)**
   - Start LIGHT — 8–10 kg DBs in Week 1. The goal in early weeks is pattern, not load. Hinge at the hip (imagine a rod through your hips and the wall behind you), keep the back perfectly neutral, feel the hamstrings load. The bar traces the legs. Stop when you feel maximum hamstring stretch (usually just below the knee). Do not round the lower back.

4. **Clamshell + Lateral Band Walk**
   - These are non-negotiable for knee health. The lateral band walk in particular directly strengthens the gluteus medius — the muscle that controls knee alignment. Use a light-to-medium band above the knees. 15 steps each direction.

#### Core Finisher (McGill-Safe)

- **McGill curl-up** — 3×8: NOT a sit-up. Lie on your back. Place hands under the lumbar curve (you should feel a natural arch there — keep it). Curl only the head and shoulders slightly off the floor. Hold 8–10 sec. This trains anterior core without compressing the lumbar discs.
- **Bird dog** — 3×8/side: On hands and knees. Extend one arm and the opposite leg simultaneously. Keep the spine absolutely still — no hip rotation, no sagging. Pause 3 sec at the top.
- **Side plank** — 3×25–30 sec/side: From the forearm. Hips stacked. If this is too difficult, modify with the bottom knee on the floor.

#### Pilates Finisher (10 min)

- **Dead bug** — 2×8/side: Lie on your back, arms to ceiling, hips and knees at 90°. Lower one arm and the opposite leg toward the floor simultaneously while maintaining back contact with the mat. Your Pilates background means you will understand the "imprint" position here.
- **Single-leg stretch** — 2×10: Classic Pilates. Curl the head and shoulders up, draw one knee to chest, extend the other leg.
- **Spine stretch forward** — 1×8: Seated, legs extended. Round forward from the crown of the head. Decompress the spine.

### Day B — Upper Body + Turkish Get-Up
**Tuesday, 7:30–8:50 am**
**Purpose:** Pull and push patterns, TGU for full-body integration, shoulder health, Pilates core.
**Movement patterns:** Pull, Push, Full-body (TGU), Core.

#### Warm-Up (7 min)

- **Shoulder CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations)** — 5 slow circles/arm: Move through the full rotator cuff range deliberately.
- **Band pull-apart** — 2×20: Hold a light band at chest height, arms wide, pull it apart until it touches the chest. Activates rear deltoids and rotator cuff.
- **Cat-cow** — 10 reps
- **Arm circles + wrist circles** — 10 sec each direction

#### Main Work

1. **Turkish Get-Up (TGU)**
   - Begin the session with TGU while your nervous system is fresh — technique breaks down when fatigued.
   - Start with 4–6 kg. Three reps per side, moving slowly.
   - The 6-phase sequence: (1) Roll to press — extend the arm, lock the elbow; (2) Roll to elbow — keep eyes on the bell; (3) Post to hand — press up through the shoulder; (4) Hip bridge — drive the hip high; (5) Sweep the leg through to the kneeling position; (6) Stand. Reverse for the descent.
   - Your yoga and Pilates background is a genuine advantage here. The body awareness required — the breath, the segmental control, the shoulder packing — are all skills you have been training for three years. TGU will feel more familiar than it does to most people who begin it. Be patient with the sequence and trust that fluency will come within two to three sessions.

2. **Single-arm DB Row**
   - Place one knee and the same-side hand on a chair or box for support. Row the DB up toward the hip, not the shoulder. Feel the lat engage. Avoid rotating the torso. Heavy is fine here once the pattern is established.

3. **Push-up progression**
   - Progress in this order: mat incline push-up (hands elevated) → standard push-up → archer push-up (one arm wider, loading shifts to that side).
   - Choose the variation where you can complete all reps with perfect form: body in a straight line from heel to crown, elbows at roughly 45° from the torso, chest touches the surface.

4. **DB Floor Press**
   - Lie on the floor, DBs at chest height, elbows at 45°. Press to full extension. The floor limits shoulder ROM at the bottom — this makes it safer than a full bench press for people building shoulder strength, and the reduced ROM is protective for anyone with anterior shoulder sensitivity.

5. **Band Face Pull**
   - Anchor a band to a door frame or sturdy furniture at face height. Pull toward the nose with elbows high and wide. Finish with external rotation (hands pull apart slightly at the end range). This is the most important shoulder health exercise in the programme. Do it every upper body session without exception.

#### Pilates Finisher (10 min)

- **The Hundred** — 1×100 pumps: Lie on back, legs at 45° (or tabletop if needed), arms pumping by the sides. 5 pumps inhale, 5 pumps exhale. Maintain the curl of the head and shoulders.
- **Single-leg stretch** — 2×10
- **Spine stretch + Swan** — 2×8 each: Spine stretch rounds forward; Swan extends from prone — the combination works the spine in both directions.

### Day C — Zone 2 Rucking + Conditioning Circuit
**Thursday, 7:30–9:15 am**
**Purpose:** Cardiovascular health, Zone 2 aerobic base, metabolic conditioning, active recovery for joints.
**Movement patterns:** Cardiovascular, Hinge, Push, Pull, Squat.

#### Rucking (35–40 min)

- **Load**: Start at 5 kg; increase to 7 kg by Week 3.
- **Pace**: Zone 2 — you should be able to speak in full sentences. Target heart rate: 108–126 bpm for age 40 (60–70% of HRmax). Use the talk test if you do not have a heart rate monitor.
- **Route**: Neighbourhood loop or park. Start before 9 am to avoid Singapore heat and humidity.
- **Posture**: Upright, shoulders back, pack sitting high on the back (not hanging low). Engage the core lightly throughout.
- **Why rucking specifically**: Unlike running, rucking is low-impact on the knees while still providing a vertical compressive force on the skeleton — a key stimulus for maintaining bone density. For women approaching perimenopause, where oestrogen decline accelerates bone loss, this is a meaningful benefit. It also burns significantly more calories than walking (~2–3× more per hour) without the injury risk of running.

#### Post-Ruck Conditioning Circuit (20 min, 2 rounds)

Perform back-to-back with 90 sec rest between rounds. This is a metabolic finisher, not a strength session — keep weights moderate.

- KB Swing: ×12
- Push-up (or incline): ×10
- Single-arm DB Row: ×10/side
- Goblet Squat: ×10
- Rest 90 sec

#### Mobility Finisher (10 min — addresses root causes of knee and lower back pain)

- **90/90 hip mobility** — 2 min: Sit in the 90/90 position (one hip internally rotated, one externally). Work through the end range gently.
- **World's greatest stretch** — 3 reps/side: Lunge forward, place the same-side hand on the floor, rotate the opposite arm to the ceiling. Combines hip flexor stretch, thoracic rotation, and hamstring lengthening.
- **Couch stretch (kneeling hip flexor)** — 60 sec/side: Rear foot on a sofa or chair, front leg at 90°. This targets the rectus femoris and psoas. Tight psoas pulls the lumbar spine into excessive extension (anterior pelvic tilt), which is one of the most common contributing factors to lower back pain. This stretch should be a daily habit.
- **Foam roller T-spine** — 60 sec: Roller perpendicular to the spine at three levels (lower thoracic, mid thoracic, upper thoracic). Arms crossed over chest. Gently extend over the roller. Do not roll the lower back.

### Day D — Full Body Strength + Carry
**Friday, 7:30–8:50 am**
**Purpose:** Full-body integration, loaded carry, single-leg strength, anti-rotation core.
**Movement patterns:** Power/Full-body, Single-Leg, Pull, Carry, Core.

#### Warm-Up (7 min)

- **Hip hinge drill (bodyweight)** — 2×10: Stand with feet hip-width. Push hips back as far as possible while keeping the back flat. Touch fingers to mid-shin. Stand and repeat. Feel the hamstrings load.
- **Thoracic rotation in quadruped** — 10/side: On hands and knees, one hand behind the head, rotate the elbow to the ceiling. Warms up the thoracic spine for the pressing movement.
- **Glute bridge** — 2×15
- **Band pull-apart** — 2×15

#### Main Work

1. **KB Single-arm Clean + Press**
   - Start with 8 kg. The clean: hinge and "hike" the KB, then aggressively pull the elbow high as the hips extend, "catching" the KB in the rack position (bell resting on the forearm, elbow tucked to ribs). From rack: press straight overhead to full lockout. Lower back to rack, then hinge and park the KB.
   - This is a full-body power chain exercise — hips generate the energy, the shoulder completes it. It is excellent for fat loss because it demands coordination, power, and stability simultaneously.

2. **Bulgarian Split Squat**
   - Rear foot elevated on a chair behind you, DBs held at sides. Lower the back knee toward the floor under control. Depth to comfortable range — do not push through knee discomfort. The front foot does the work. This is the most challenging single-leg exercise in the programme, so start with light DBs (or bodyweight) and earn the load gradually.

3. **DB Bent-over Row**
   - Both arms simultaneously. Hinge at the hip (maintain the back angle throughout the set), pull the DBs to the lower rib cage. This can be loaded heavier than the single-arm row because you have bilateral stability.

4. **KB or DB Farmer Carry**
   - 3×20 m. Measure out a corridor or garden path. Hold a KB or DB in each hand, stand tall, brace the core, and walk. This exercise is deceptively hard and carries outsized benefits: grip strength, core bracing under load, posture, and trap development.

5. **Band Pallof Press**
   - Anchor a band to a door frame or sturdy furniture at chest height. Stand sideways to the anchor. Hold the band at chest with both hands. Press directly forward to full arm extension, hold 2 sec, return. The band tries to rotate you — resist it. This is anti-rotation core training, which is more functional than most traditional ab exercises.

#### Cool-Down (10 min)

- **Foam roller**: IT band (side of thigh), quads, glutes — key for knee maintenance.
- **90/90 hip** — 1 min/side
- **Child's pose + thoracic extension** — hold each 30–60 sec

## 6. The 4-Week Undulating Periodisation

| Week | Phase | Sets | Reps | RPE | Notes |
|------|-------|------|------|-----|-------|
| Week 1 | Technique + Accumulation | 3 | 10 | 6–7 | Master movement patterns. Lighter loads. Feel comfortable with TGU and swings. The goal this week is not fatigue — it is fluency. |
| Week 2 | Build | 4 | 8 | 7–8 | Increase loads. Technique should be solid. Add one set across most exercises. You should feel challenged but not crushed. |
| Week 3 | Volume | 4 | 10–12 | 7–8 | More total reps. Metabolic stress. Slightly lighter than Week 2 per set, but more total volume. Expect to feel this in the 48 hours after sessions. |
| Week 4 | Deload | 2 | 10 | 5 | 50% volume. Same exercises. Light and technical. This is not a "lazy week" — it is when adaptation consolidates. Use extra time for mobility work. |

## 8. Progressive Overload Guidelines

**When to add weight:**
Complete all prescribed sets and reps at RPE ≤6 for two consecutive sessions → increase weight. For dumbbells: add 1–2 kg. For kettlebells: move to the next bell size. Do not chase weight — chase technique.

**Knee rule:**
If goblet squat causes knee pain during or after the session, reduce depth to the point that is pain-free. If pain persists at any depth, substitute a box squat: sit back onto a chair and stand. This removes the need to control the descent and is a safe bridge exercise while the knee is sensitive.

**Lower back rule:**
If the RDL causes lower back pain, reduce weight significantly and revisit the hip hinge pattern. Film yourself from the side if possible. If pain persists despite lightening the load, substitute the hip thrust: sit your back against a sofa, feet flat, drive the hips up to full extension. The hip thrust is glute-dominant and places minimal load on the lumbar spine.

**TGU rule:**
Do not increase KB weight until you can complete all 6 phases on both sides with smooth, unhurried movement. Rushing the TGU with a weight you cannot control defeats its purpose. The movement should feel like a deliberate sequence, not a struggle.

**Deload rule:**
Take a deload week every fourth week regardless of how you feel. The adaptations from hard training occur during recovery, not during the sessions themselves.

## 9. Fat Loss Tracking

### Weekly Weigh-In Protocol
- Weigh every Sunday morning, post-void, before eating or drinking
- Wear minimal or no clothing for consistency
- Log the number — do not react emotionally to a single reading; look for the trend over 3–4 weeks

### Expected Rate of Loss
- **Target: 0.3–0.5 kg/week** — this is the sustainable, muscle-preserving range
- 0.3 kg/week = ~100 g fat lost per day at the cellular level — genuinely meaningful over a month
- Over this 4-week programme: expect 1.2–2.0 kg of fat loss if the plan is followed consistently

### Adjustments

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Losing >0.7 kg/week | Add 100 kcal/day — risk of muscle loss at faster rate. Add to breakfast (extra egg or slightly more milk). |
| No loss after 2 full weeks | Reduce lunch carbs by half: ¼ cup brown rice instead of ½ cup. This is the first and simplest lever to pull. |
| Scale not moving but measurements decreasing | Continue as-is — this is body recomposition (fat loss + muscle gain). Common in the first 4–6 weeks of a new programme. |
| Very hungry on rest days | Add a small protein snack (100 g cottage cheese or 1 hard-boiled egg) — this is preferable to eating off-plan. |

### Monthly Measurements
Take on Day 1 and Day 28, same time of day (morning, before eating):
- **Waist**: at the narrowest point, just above the navel
- **Hip**: at the widest point across the buttocks
- **Thigh**: at the mid-thigh of the dominant leg, measured from the same anatomical landmark each time

Scale weight alone misses the most important changes happening in your body. A month of consistent training and appropriate nutrition will almost certainly produce visible changes in measurements and how clothes fit, even if the scale moves less than expected (especially in the early weeks as muscles adapt and glycogen stores shift).

## Notes for Long-Term Progress

This 4-week plan is designed to be **repeatable with progressive loading**. After Week 4 (deload), begin again from Week 1 of the same structure — but with heavier loads than you started this cycle. Every 4 weeks you will be measurably stronger than the previous cycle.

Three months of consistent application of this plan — with honest nutrition adherence and progressive loading — will produce a transformation in both body composition and physical capacity that three more years of yoga and Pilates alone would not. The combination of resistance training, adequate protein, modest caloric deficit, and Zone 2 cardio is one of the most evidence-based approaches to body composition improvement in the scientific literature for women.

The knee and lower back issues you experience are not a barrier — they are a signal. This programme addresses their root causes (weak glutes, tight hip flexors, poor anterior core endurance) directly. Most people with similar presentations find that pain frequency and severity decrease significantly within 6–8 weeks of consistent hip/glute work and McGill core training.

Finally: the Sunday family swim is not optional. It is one of the best things in this programme.
